# Financial Accounting, 9th edition - Chapter 2
- Basic equation:

$Assets = Liabilities + Equity$

## The Account
- `account`: an individual accounting record of increases and decreases in a specific asset, liability, or equity item.
  - Three parts
    - 1. Title
    - 2. Left or debit side (Dr) => crediting
    - 3. Right or credit side (Cr) => debiting
  - T-account
  - Balance:
    - debit balance if Dr > Cr
    - credit balance if Cr > Dr
- **double-entry system**: each transaction must affect two or more accounts to keep the basic accounting equation in balance.
  - For each transaction, debits must equal credits.

## DR./CR. procedures for assets and liabilities
- The effects that debits and credits have on assets and liabilities:

|Debits|Credits|
|:---|:---|
|⬆︎ Assets|⬇︎ Assets|
|⬇︎ Liabilities|⬆︎ Liabilities|

- Asset accounts normally show debit balances.
- Liability accounts normally show credit balances.

## Stockholders' Equity
- 5 subdivisions: common stock, retained earnings, dividends, revenues, and expenses.

### Common stock
- Companies issue common stock in exchange for the owners' investment paid to the corporation.
- Example: when an owner invests cash in the business in exchange for shares of the corporation's stock, the company debits (increases) Cash and credits (increases) Common Stock.
- The rules of debit and credit for the Common Stock account:

|Debits|Credits|
|:---|:---|
|⬇︎ Common Stock|⬆︎ Common Stock|

### Retained Earnings
- Net income that is kept in the business.
- Represents the portion of stockholders' equity that the company has accumulated through the profitable operation of the business.
- Credits (net income) increase the Retained Earnings
- Debit (dividends or net losses) decrease it

### Dividends
- A company's distribution to its stockholders on a pro rata (equal) basis.
- Most common form is a **cash dividend**.
- Dividends reduce the stockholders' claims on retained earnings.
- Debits increase the Dividends account, and credits decrease it.

### Revenues and Expenses
- The purpose of earning revenues is to benefit the stockholders of the business.
- Credits increase revenue accounts and debits decrease them.
- Expenses have the opposite effect.
  - Decrease stockholders' equity.
  - Increased by debits and decreased by credits.

## The Journal
- Companies initially record transactions in chronological order.
- The `journal` is referred to as the book of original entry.
- For each transaction, the journal shows the debit and credit effects on specific accounts.

### Journalizing
- Entering transaction data in the journal.
- Separate journal entries for each transaction.
- A complete entry consists of:
  - 1. The date of the transaction
  - 2. The accounts and amoounts to be debited and credited
  - 3. A brief explanation of the transaction.

- Example: General Journal (J1)

|Date|Account Titles and Explanation|Ref|Debit|Credit|
|:---|:---|:---:|:---:|:---:|
|2015 Sep 1|Cash||15,000||
||__Common Stock|||15,000|
||____(Issued shares of stock for cash)||||
|2015 Sep 1|Equipment||7,000||
||__Cash|||7,000|
||____(Purchase of equipment for cash)||||

- The debit account title (the account to be debited) is entered first at the extreme left margin of the column
- The credit account title (the account to be credited) is indented and entered on the next line
- A brief explanatioin of the transaction appears on the line below the credit account title.
- A blank space spearates individual journal entries
- The column titled Ref is left blank when the journal entry is made. This column is used later when the journal entries are transferred to the ledger accounts.

### Simple and compound entries
- Example: General Journal (J1)

|Date|Account Titles and Explanation|Ref|Debit|Credit|
|:---|:---|:---:|---:|---:|
|2015 Jul 1|Equipment||14,000||
||__Cash|||8,000|
||__Accounts Payable|||6,000|
||____(Purchased truck for cash with balance on account)||||

- In a compound entry, the standard format requires that all debits be listed before the credits.

## The Ledger
- The entire group of accounts maintained by a company is the `ledger`.
- It provides the balance in each of the accounts as well as keeps track of changes in these balances.
- A **general ledger** contains all the asset, liability, and stockholders' equity accounts.
- Example:
  - Individual Asset Accounts: Equipment, Land, Supplies, Cash
  - Individual Liability Accounts: Interest Payable, Salaries and Wages Payable, Accounts Payable, Notes Payable
  - Individual Stockholders' Equity Accounts: Salaries and Wages Expense, Service Revenue, Dividends, Retained Earning, Common Stock

- Companies arrange the ledger in the sequence in  which they present the accounts in the financial statements.
  - Beginning with the balance sheet accounts.
  - First in order are the asset accounts
  - Followed by liability accounts
  - Then Stockholders' equity accounts, revenues, and expenses
- Each account is numbered for easier identification

- The balance in the account is determined after each transaction.

## Posting
- Transferring journal entries to the ledger accounts.
- Accumulates the effects of journalized transactions into the individual accounts.
- Steps:
  - 1. In the **ledger**, in the appropriate columns of the account(s) debited, enter the date, journal page, and debit amount shown in the journal.
  - 2. In the reference column of the **journal**, write the account number to which the debit amount was posted.
  - 3. In the **ledger**, in the appropriate columns of the account(s) credited, enter the date, journal page, and credit amount shown in the journal.
  - 4. In the reference column of the **journal**, write the account number to which the credit amount was posted.
- Posting should be performed in chronological order.
- The reference column of a ledger account indicates the journal page from which the transaction was posted.
- The explanation space of the ledger account is used infrequently because an explanation already appears in the journal.

### Use of Dollar Signs
- Do not appear in journals or ledgers.
- Used only in the trial balance and the financial statements.
- Generally, shown only for the first item in the colum and for the total of that column.
- A single line (a totaling rule) is placed under the column of figures to be added or subtraced
- Total amounts are double-underlined to indicate they are final sums.

# Notes
- A financial transaction ledger system is the core record-keeping system used in accounting to track all financial activity.
- At its heart is the **general ledger**, which stores transactionsin accounts such as Cash, Accounts Receivable, Revenue, Expenses, and Loans.

## How it works
### 1. A transaction occurs
- For example, a business sells a product for $100

### 2. The transaction is recorded using double-entry accounting
- Modern ledger systems typically use **double-entry bookkeeping**, where every transaction affects at least two accounts and must remain balanced.
- The total debits must equal the total credits.
- For the $100 sale:

|Account|Debit|Credit|
|:---|:---|:---|
|Cash|100||
|Revenue||100|

- Cash increases, so it is debited.
- Revenue increases, so it is credited.
- The ledger remains balanced because total debits ($100) equal total credits ($100).

### 3. Entires are posted to ledger accounts
- Each account maintains its own history of transactions.
  - The Cash account records the increase
  - The Revenue account records the income.
- Ledger accounts are the principal records from which financial statements are produced.

### 4. Balances are updated
- After posting, the system recalculates account balances.
- Businesses can then determine:
  - How much cash they have
  - What customers owe them
  - What they owe others
  - Their revenue and expenses
  - Their overall financial position
- These balances ultimately feed into financial statements.

## Key characteristics of a good ledger system
- **Completeness**: records every financial transaction.
- **Balance**: debits equal credits for every posting.
- **Auditability**: transactions can be traced back to their source.
- **Accuracy**: helps detect errors because unbalanced entires are not allowed.
- **Financial reporting**: provides the data used to prepare balance sheets, income statement and other reports.

## Main outputs of a general ledger
### 1. Trial Balance
- Lists all ledger accounts and their ending balances at a specific date.
- Example:

|Account|Balance|
|:---|:---|
|Cash|$10,000.00|
|Accounts Receivable|$5,000.00|
|Revenue|$20,000.00|
|Expenses|$12,000.00|

- The total debits should equal total credits, helping identify posting errors.

### 2. Income Statement (Profit & Loss Statement)
- Shows financial performance over a period
  - Revenue
  - Cost of sales
  - Operating expenses
  - Net profit or loss
- The figures come from revenue and expense accounts held in the general ledger.

### 3. Balance sheet
- Show the organization's financial position at a point in time:
  - Assets
  - Liabilities
  - Equity
- These balances are derived from the corresponding ledger accounts.

### 4. Cash Flow Statement
- Summarizes cash inflows and outflows, helping stakeholders understand:
  - Operating cash flow
  - Investing cash flow
  - Financing cash flow
- This statement is prepared using information recorded in the ledger.

### 5. Account Balance Reports
- A ledger system can produce detailed balances for individual accounts, such as:
  - Cash balances
  - Customer receivable balances
  - Vendor payable balances
  - Loan balances
- Each account contains a history of transactions and current balance.

### 6. Transaction History and Audit Trail
- The ledger provides a chronological record of all postings, allowing users to trace:
  - Who recorded the transaction
  - When it was recorded
  - Which accounts were affected
- This supports auditing, reconciliation, and compliance activities.

## Simple Flow
```
Business Transactions
         ↓
     Journal Entries
         ↓
     General Ledger
         ↓
    Trial Balance
         ↓
  Financial Statements
   • Income Statement
   • Balance Sheet
   • Cash Flow Statement
```
